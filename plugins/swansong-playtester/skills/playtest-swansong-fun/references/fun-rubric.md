# SwanSong Fun Rubric

Use the rubric after the discovery, competence, and replay passes. Scores are
reviewer judgments anchored to observed play, not measurements of universal
taste.

## Common scale

| Score | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | The dimension blocks or seriously undermines the core loop. |
| 2 | A promising element appears, but frequent friction or weak payoff dominates. |
| 3 | The dimension works and has engaging moments, with noticeable flat or unclear stretches. |
| 4 | The dimension is consistently strong and supports another attempt. |
| 5 | The dimension creates a compelling, escalating reason to keep playing or experimenting. |

## Dimensions

### Discoverability

Judge whether the title, onboarding, prompts, feedback, and first result let a
new player form a correct mental model. Penalize hidden rules only when the
discovery process is unrewarding or feedback cannot support a useful hypothesis.

### Control feel and feedback

Judge visible and audible response, action legibility, timing, state changes,
and recovery from mistakes. Separate intentional commitment from input delay.

### Decision quality

Judge how often the player makes a meaningful choice with understandable
tradeoffs. Repeated execution can still score well when precision, timing, or
route planning changes the outcome; repeated low-value confirmation cannot.

### Momentum and tension

Judge the rhythm from start to first payoff, escalation toward a result, dead
time, interruption cost, and whether pressure grows in a readable way.

### Learning and mastery

Judge whether knowledge or skill from one attempt improves the next. Look for
better predictions, cleaner execution, new strategies, and satisfying recovery.

### Variety and surprise

Judge whether later states, alternate choices, audiovisual responses, and
system interactions create meaningful novelty without obscuring the rules.

### Replay pull

Judge whether the observed ending or result creates a specific reason for an
immediate second attempt: improve a score, try another route, reach a distinct
ending, test a hypothesis, solve more efficiently, or explore another setup.

## Genre emphasis

- **Action and racing:** emphasize control feel, readable danger, recovery,
  escalation, and restart cost.
- **Puzzle and tactics:** emphasize decision quality, hypothesis feedback,
  solution space, turn pace, and mastery.
- **Audio-led play:** emphasize cue timing, stereo legibility, channel priority,
  and whether listening changes decisions.
- **Utility or editor loops:** emphasize experimentation depth, reversible
  actions, state visibility, and the reward for trying another configuration.

Do not change the seven dimensions between games. Genre emphasis changes the
explanation and recommendation, not the scale.

## Optional evidence metrics

Record these only when exact frame evidence supports them:

- frames from boot to an actionable prompt;
- frames from start to the first meaningful decision, reward, and setback;
- longest stretch of repeated input without a new decision;
- frames from result to a clean replay;
- distinct strategies, outcomes, or configurations observed; and
- number of recoverable versus unrecoverable mistakes exercised.

Never estimate missing frame counts or convert an emulator frame count into a
human reaction-time claim.

## Overall outlook

- **Strong:** several dimensions consistently reinforce the core loop, replay
  pull is clear, and no severe friction dominates the tested route.
- **Mixed:** the game produces real spark moments, but one or more recurring
  problems weaken momentum, clarity, agency, or the desire to replay.
- **Weak:** the tested core loop provides little readable agency or payoff, or
  severe friction prevents its promising elements from becoming accessible.

Always pair the outlook with `high`, `medium`, or `low` confidence. Confidence
depends on completed passes, outcomes reached, alternate paths exercised, and
audio coverage—not on how strongly the reviewer phrases the verdict.
